Polynomials and Rational Functions

Understanding polynomials and rational functions is crucial for solving TMUA questions. This section covers:

  • Basic definitions and operations
  • Factoring polynomials
  • Graphing polynomials
  • Solving polynomial equations
  • Rational functions and asymptotes

Basic Definitions and Operations

Polynomials are expressions consisting of variables and coefficients, involving only the operations of addition, subtraction, multiplication, and non-negative integer exponents of variables.

  • Definition: A polynomial is an expression like \( ax^n + bx^{n-1} + ... + k \), where \(a, b, ..., k\) are constants and \(n\) is a non-negative integer.

    Example: \( P(x) = 4x^3 - 3x^2 + 2x - 1 \) is a polynomial of degree 3.
    Explanation: The highest power of x is 3, so the degree is 3. The coefficients are 4, -3, 2, and -1.
  • Addition and Subtraction: Combine like terms by adding or subtracting their coefficients.

    Example: Simplify \( (3x^2 + 2x - 1) + (2x^2 - x + 4) \)
    Step 1: Identify like terms: \(3x^2\) and \(2x^2\), \(2x\) and \(-x\), -1 and 4
    Step 2: Add the coefficients of like terms: \((3+2)x^2 + (2-1)x + (-1+4)\)
    Step 3: Simplify: \(5x^2 + x + 3\)

Factoring Polynomials

Factoring involves expressing a polynomial as a product of its factors.

  • Common Factor: Identify and factor out the greatest common factor (GCF) of all terms.

    Example: Factor \( 6x^2 - 12x \)
    Step 1: Identify the GCF: 6x is common to both terms
    Step 2: Factor out the GCF: \( 6x(x - 2) \)
  • Difference of Squares: For expressions in the form \(a^2 - b^2\), factor as \((a+b)(a-b)\).

    Example: Factor \( x^2 - 4 \)
    Step 1: Recognize this as a difference of squares: \(x^2 - 2^2\)
    Step 2: Apply the formula: \((x+2)(x-2)\)

Graphing Polynomials

Graphing involves plotting the polynomial on a coordinate system to visualize its behavior.

  • Intercepts: Find where the polynomial crosses the x and y axes.

    Example: Find the intercepts of \( y = x^2 - 4x + 3 \)
    For x-intercepts: Set y = 0 and solve \( x^2 - 4x + 3 = 0 \)
    Factor: \( (x-1)(x-3) = 0 \), so x = 1 or x = 3
    For y-intercept: Set x = 0, y = 3
    Intercepts are (1,0), (3,0), and (0,3)
  • End Behavior: Analyze how the function behaves as x approaches positive or negative infinity.

    Example: Determine the end behavior of \( y = x^3 - 2x^2 + x - 1 \)
    Step 1: Identify the leading term: \(x^3\)
    Step 2: As x → ∞, \(y → ∞\) (cubic function with positive leading coefficient)
    Step 3: As x → -∞, \(y → -∞\)

Solving Polynomial Equations

Solving involves finding the roots of the polynomial equation.

  • Factoring Method: Factor the polynomial and set each factor to zero.

    Example: Solve \( x^3 - x^2 - 6x = 0 \)
    Step 1: Factor out the GCF: \( x(x^2 - x - 6) = 0 \)
    Step 2: Factor further: \( x(x+2)(x-3) = 0 \)
    Step 3: Set each factor to zero: x = 0, x = -2, or x = 3

Rational Functions and Asymptotes

Rational functions are ratios of polynomials. Asymptotes are lines that the graph of the function approaches but never touches.

  • Vertical Asymptotes: Occur where the denominator equals zero.

    Example: Find the vertical asymptote of \( f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 1}{x - 2} \)
    Step 1: Set the denominator to zero: x - 2 = 0
    Step 2: Solve for x: x = 2
    The vertical asymptote is at x = 2
  • Horizontal Asymptotes: Determined by comparing the degrees of the numerator and denominator.

    Example: Find the horizontal asymptote of \( f(x) = \frac{2x^2 + 3x - 1}{x^2 - 4} \)
    Step 1: Compare degrees: both numerator and denominator are degree 2
    Step 2: Divide the leading coefficients: 2 ÷ 1 = 2
    The horizontal asymptote is y = 2

Interactive TMUA Style Question

Now, apply what you've learned to this TMUA-style question:

Question:

Find the roots of the polynomial \( x^3 - 6x^2 + 11x - 6 = 0 \).

A) 1, 2, 4
B) 1, 2, 3
C) 2, 3, 4
D) 1, 3, 4

Explanation:

The roots of the polynomial \( x^3 - 6x^2 + 11x - 6 \) can be found by factoring. First, try possible rational roots using the Rational Root Theorem. Testing \( x = 1 \), \( x = 2 \), and \( x = 3 \), we find that \( x = 1 \), \( x = 2 \), and \( x = 3 \) are roots. Thus, the polynomial factors as \( (x - 1)(x - 2)(x - 3) = 0 \).
